\begin{abstract}
This project builds an end-to-end football video analysis system from broadcast footage.
The pipeline combines a fine-tuned YOLOv8 detector, persistent multi-object tracking,
team assignment from jersey colour clustering, and ball interpolation / ROI recovery to
improve ball coverage. Quantitatively, the fine-tuned full pipeline detects the ball in
91.7\% of frames, compared with 43.6\% for a generic baseline, while also reducing player
track fragmentation from 175 tracks to 50 and increasing average track length from 101.9 to
305.7 frames.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Football broadcast analysis is a useful but challenging video understanding task. The scene
contains small and fast-moving objects, frequent occlusions, camera motion, and visually similar
players from opposing teams. The objective of this project is to estimate object locations and
derive higher-level match statistics from a single match video.

The deep learning component of the project is based on representation learning for object
detection: a YOLOv8 detector is fine-tuned on football-specific annotations for four classes
(ball, player, goalkeeper, referee). The detector output is then consumed by a tracking and
analysis pipeline that converts per-frame predictions into stable trajectories, team identities,
and ball possession signals.

\section{Related Work}
Object detection and multi-object tracking are standard building blocks in modern sports analytics.
YOLO-style detectors are widely used because they provide a good trade-off between accuracy and
speed, while ByteTrack-style trackers help maintain stable identities across frames.

In football, the most difficult targets are the ball and the referee, due to their small size,
motion blur, and frequent occlusion. This project therefore focuses on a practical combination of
deep detection, heuristics, and temporal smoothing rather than on a single monolithic model.

\section{Methodology}

\subsection{Detection with YOLOv8}
The detector is based on YOLOv8 and was fine-tuned on the football dataset to predict the four
target classes. Fine-tuning helps the model adapt to the broadcast viewpoint, the jersey patterns,
and the small-object scale of the ball.

Baseline configuration: generic pretrained YOLO weights, minimal post-processing, no team
assignment, no interpolation, and no camera compensation.

Full configuration: fine-tuned football weights, tracking, ball interpolation, ball ROI recovery,
and team assignment.

\subsection{Tracking}
Detection outputs are associated across frames using a ByteTrack-style tracker. Tracking converts
per-frame detections into longer-lived identities and makes it possible to compute trajectory-based
metrics such as track length and track stability.

\subsection{Team Assignment}
Team identity is inferred from jersey-colour descriptors extracted from player crops. The approach
clusters colour features and propagates a team label per track. This is not a learned classifier,
but a lightweight visual inference module that sits on top of the detector/tracker output.

\subsection{Ball Interpolation and ROI Recovery}
The ball is the hardest class to detect consistently. Short gaps are filled with linear
interpolation, and a ball-specific ROI recovery pass revisits the last known region when the ball is
missed by the global detector. This improves coverage without changing the detector weights.

\subsection{Camera Motion and Perspective}
Camera motion compensation can be estimated with optical flow on static regions of the pitch. In a
more complete version of the pipeline, these corrections can be combined with a perspective
transform to convert image coordinates into approximate field coordinates.

\subsection{Metrics}
The experiment framework stores per-frame and aggregated metrics. The main metrics are:
frames with detection per class, average detections per frame, total tracks, average and maximum
track length, ball observed versus interpolated frames, maximum interpolation gap, team switch
counts, and ball coverage percentage.

\section{Discussion}
The gap between the baseline and the fine-tuned model is consistent with the expectations of deep
representation learning. The domain-specific model learns football-specific features such as jersey
patterns, pitch context, and the visual appearance of the ball under broadcast conditions.

Ball interpolation is a useful temporal prior: it does not invent new information, but it smooths
short occlusion gaps and makes the final trajectory more complete. In contrast, camera motion
compensation and team assignment primarily affect the interpretability and coherence of the
analysis outputs rather than the raw ball coverage.

Typical failure modes include the ball being airborne, extreme motion blur, partial occlusions near
clusters of players, and visually ambiguous goalkeepers. These errors are tied to object scale,
appearance variability, and the limitations of a frame-based detector.

\section{Limitations and Future Work}
The evaluation is based on a limited number of broadcast videos and a single camera viewpoint.
Future work could include more ball examples, improved small-object detection, temporal models,
multi-view calibration, and stronger camera-motion compensation.

\section{Conclusion}
This project demonstrates a practical football analysis pipeline that combines YOLOv8 detection,
multi-object tracking, lightweight team inference, and ball recovery heuristics. The fine-tuned model
substantially improves ball coverage and tracking stability over a generic baseline, which makes it a
strong foundation for higher-level football analytics.
